# Template 10 — Social Proof

**Reference Image:** `10_Social_Proof.png`
**Carousel Slot:** Typically Image 7 or 8. Near the end of the carousel, after the customer has learned what the product is and does — now they need confirmation that others have already made this decision.

---

## What This Image Must Do

Social Proof does one thing: it shows the customer they're not taking a risk. Other people — large numbers of them, and credible publications — have already decided this product is worth it. The customer who scrolls to this image is already interested. They just need permission to buy.

The key device is media credibility: the "As Seen On" logo bar with recognizable press outlets. When a customer sees BuzzFeed, People, The Hollywood Reporter, or MarketWatch in a logo bar, their brain shortcuts past scrutiny and moves to trust. Earned media coverage is a third-party endorsement that no product claim can replicate.

The headline in this template is tonally different from every other template. It's permitted to be playful, punny, or cheeky. "CRITICS GIVE IT TWO TOES UP" is a perfect example — it's a wordplay on "two thumbs up" adapted to a foot care product. This lightness is intentional. By the time the customer reaches the Social Proof image, they've absorbed the functional information. Now the brand can relax and have a personality.

---

## Typography

| Element | Font | Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Header | Neuzeit Grotesk ExtCond Black | Scaled to layout | Two-tone format. Playful tone permitted. Short — the pun or witty phrase must land fast. |
| "As Seen On:" Label | Poppins SemiBold | Smaller — positioned above the logo bar | This is the functional label. It doesn't need to be large — the logos are the content. |

### Header Two-Tone Rule
Applies here, but with a different character than other templates. In the reference image: "CRITICS GIVE IT" in dark neutral (navy), "TWO TOES UP" in the product accent (sky blue). The pun is in the accent phrase — the dark neutral phrase is the setup, the accent phrase is the punchline. This structure rewards the two-tone convention by making the color break land where the humor is.

The header in this template can be:
- A pun or wordplay adapted to the product category ("Two Toes Up" for foot care, "Hands Down the Best" for gloves)
- A milestone stat headline ("1.8 MILLION SOCKS SOLD")
- A direct endorsement frame ("THE PRESS AGREES")

Whatever form it takes, it should be short enough to read in one visual sweep and memorable enough to stick.

---

## Layout Structure

This template has a cleaner, more editorial structure than the data-heavy templates. It reads top-to-bottom.

**Top Zone — Header**
- The header occupies a strong color block at the top of the canvas. In the reference image this is a deep navy band — the header text sits in cream/white, with the accent phrase in the product accent color.
- The sparkle brand graphic (typically 2 sparkles from the set, not the full 3-sparkle cluster) appears beside or after the header — typically at the end of the last line. In the reference image, the sparkles appear to the right of "TWO TOES UP" — they function as a visual exclamation point.
- The color block that holds the header can be deep navy, or the product accent — whichever creates stronger contrast with the logo bar zone below.

**Middle Zone — "As Seen On" Logo Bar**
- Below the header block, on a white or light background, the "As Seen On:" label appears in Poppins SemiBold, smaller text.
- Beneath the label: a row (or two rows) of recognizable press/media logos. These should be actual branded logos — BuzzFeed, MarketWatch, Travel Noire, People, The Hollywood Reporter, HuffPost.
- The logos should be in their original brand colors or in a consistent gray treatment — not colorized to the product palette. Media logos have their own equity; applying the product color palette to them weakens both.
- Two rows of logos is ideal if there are 5+ outlets. One row of 3–4 is cleaner if fewer outlets are available.
- Never fabricate press coverage. Only use logos for publications that have actually featured the product.

**Bottom Zone — Product Image**
- Below the logo bar, the product image appears. It's large and clean — either a cutout of the product itself or a tight lifestyle crop.
- In the reference image, a pair of feet with blue-painted toenails extends from the bottom of the canvas into the logo bar zone. This is a deliberate framing choice — the product (worn on the feet) is implied rather than shown as packaging.
- The product image here is lighter in visual weight than in other templates — the logos have already done the persuasive work. The product simply confirms what was earned.

## Design Elements Required

| Element | Placement | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Header Color Block | Top of canvas | Deep navy or product accent fill. Header text inside. |
| Sparkle Accent | Adjacent to header text — end of headline or upper corner | 1–2 sparkles from the brand graphic system. Used as punctuation rather than the full 3-sparkle cluster. |
| "As Seen On:" Label | Above the logo row | Poppins SemiBold. Small. Functional, not decorative. |
| Media Logos | Logo bar section | Actual branded logos. Black, grayscale, or original colors. Not product-colored. |
| Product Image | Bottom zone | Clean product or lifestyle crop. Not competing with the logos. |

---

## Color Guidance

- **Header block fill:** Deep navy (`#1d396a`) is the most common and most authoritative. Product accent is also viable for a lighter, more energetic feel.
- **Header text:** Cream (`#fef5e3`) or white for the base phrase on a dark block; product accent for the emphasis phrase.
- **Middle zone background:** White or very light cream. Media logos need a neutral field to read clearly.
- **Logo treatment:** Original brand colors or consistent grayscale treatment. Do not apply the product palette to media logos.
- **Bottom zone background:** Can match the middle zone (white/cream) or slightly tint toward the product accent — subtle.
- **Product image:** Natural coloring. No color treatment.
- **Sparkles:** Product accent color. If the header block is dark, sparkles can be cream or the lighter product accent tone.

## What Good Looks Like

In `10_Social_Proof.png`, the reference shows the social proof template with a deep navy header block at the top. "CRITICS GIVE IT" sits in white/cream; "TWO TOES UP" sits in sky blue accent, followed by two sparkle icons acting as visual exclamation marks. The "As Seen On:" label in Poppins SemiBold appears below on white background, followed by two rows of logos: BuzzFeed, MarketWatch, Travel Noire in row one; People, The Hollywood Reporter, HuffPost in row two. Below the logos, a cropped lifestyle photo of bare feet with blue-polished toenails extends into the frame — a product in use without showing the product's packaging at all.

The image reads immediately: press loves it, the product exists, and it's relevant to people like you.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Logo bar with fabricated press coverage.** Only use logos for actual earned media. This is both a legal risk and a trust risk if customers recognize the fabrication.
- **Logo bar colorized to the product palette.** Media logos have their own established brand equity. Altering them looks both amateurish and like you're claiming association you haven't earned.
- **Header without wit.** This template has the most creative latitude in the system. A dry, functional headline ("FEATURED IN PRESS") wastes the opportunity. Push for a pun, a milestone, or a memorable frame.
- **Sparkle missing.** Even in this editorial-style template, the sparkle provides brand continuity. It ties this image to the rest of the carousel.
- **Product absent.** Even a minimal lifestyle crop counts. The customer needs a product reference even in a credibility image.
- **Too many logos to read.** If you have more than 8 media logos, prioritize the most recognizable ones. A dense logo bar loses legibility at mobile size.
- **Full 3-sparkle cluster instead of 1–2 sparkles.** The Social Proof template uses sparkles more sparingly — as punctuation beside the header, not as the full brand cluster. Using all three sparkles competes with the header tone.
