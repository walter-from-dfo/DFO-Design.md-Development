# Template 09 — Problem Solution

**Reference Image:** `09_Problem_and_Solution.png`
**Carousel Slot:** Typically Image 2 or 3. Placed early enough to capture customers who feel the pain point but haven't yet been sold on the solution.

---

## What This Image Must Do

Problem Solution is the most emotionally direct template in the system. It looks the customer in the eye and says: "We know what you're dealing with. Here's what fixes it." The structure is an argument in visual form — problem on one side, solution on the other, the product as the bridge.

Done well, this template creates recognition before it creates desire. The customer sees the "before" state and feels seen. That moment of recognition — "yes, that's exactly what my heels look like" or "yes, that's the pain I have in my toes" — creates the emotional foundation that makes the product feel necessary, not just appealing.

There are three layout variants, each suited to different product stories. The choice of variant depends on whether the strongest argument is visual transformation (Before/After), product functionality (Product Benefit), or breadth of conditions addressed (Multi-Problem Grid).

### Header Two-Tone Rule
Applies here even without fixed sizing. The header names the pain point or the transformation in bold language. In the reference images: "USE THEM ALONE OR SUPERCHARGE A ROUTINE!" — "USE THEM ALONE OR" in dark neutral, "SUPERCHARGE" in accent color. And "FOOT PAIN STINKS" — "FOOT PAIN" in dark neutral, "STINKS" in accent. The accent word carries the emotional punch or the unexpected language choice.

## Layout Variants

### Variant 1 — Before / After (Transformation)

**Structure:**
- Header at the top, naming the problem or the dual-use case.
- Below: two side-by-side panels for each timeline scenario.
  - Left panel: "Before" state photo. Real customer imagery. Rough, cracked, visually challenging skin or condition. The photo should be honest but not grotesque.
  - Right panel: "After" state photo. Healed, smooth, dramatically improved. Same body part, same context.
  - Day-count badge overlaid at the top right of the panel pair: "1 Day" or "6 Days". Colored pill/tab shape in the product accent.
- Multiple before/after pairs can be stacked vertically — showing progression over time (e.g., Overnight Use at 1 Day, then Dream Routine at 6 Days).
- Footer: an instructional or brand-voice closing line — e.g., "Dr. Frederick's Instructions For Happy Heels Included!"
- "*Actual Customer Photos" disclaimer appears near or beneath the before/after photos.

**Before Photo Rules:**
- Real conditions. This is not a retouched or sanitized "before." The roughness, cracking, or redness should be visible and believable.
- Consistent lighting and crop between before and after so the comparison reads as the same person/zone.
- Desaturated slightly to feel "heavier" — the before state should feel heavier visually than the after state.

**After Photo Rules:**
- Warm, clear lighting. Skin smooth and visibly improved.
- Same crop/composition as the before. The comparison needs to be direct.
- The improvement must be dramatic but not fake. Authentic transformation only.

---

### Variant 2 — Product Benefit (Solution-Forward)

**Structure:**
- Header names the pain point.
- Large product image or in-use photo dominates the center.
- Benefit copy beneath describes what the product does mechanically — e.g., "SootheFast™ Gel retains healing moisture to maintain & repair dry cracked heels."
- A small before/after comparison appears as a circular inset (BEFORE and AFTER labels within small circles) to reference the transformation without making it the dominant element.
- Footer: colorway or variant swatches if the product comes in multiple colors — labeled "Available in [X] colors" or "Available in [X] stunning colors."
- Optional UGC disclaimer if any customer photos are used.

**This variant works when:**
- The product has a strong mechanism story (the gel, the compression, the material) that needs explaining.
- The physical transformation is subtle or requires more context to read clearly in a photo.
- The product comes in multiple colorways and the footer swatch strip reinforces variety.

---

### Variant 3 — Multi-Problem Grid (Condition Coverage)

**Structure:**
- Header names the overarching pain category — e.g., "FOOT PAIN STINKS".
- Sub line names the product and what it solves — e.g., "Be Unbeatable with Hammer Toe Gels."
- A grid of specific conditions appears below the header. Each cell shows:
  - A close-up photo of the affected body area with a visual indicator (e.g., a red glow, arrow, or outline highlighting the problem zone)
  - A label naming the specific condition — "Hammer Toes", "Claw Toes Corns", "Overlapping Toes", "Other Toe Pain"
- Product image appears within or beside the grid, as the solution.
- A badge or callout labels what the product does — "Relieves Discomfort Due To:".
- This variant works best for products that address multiple conditions (toe spacers, bunion correctors, multi-purpose insoles) because it shows breadth of coverage.

**Visual Indicator in Condition Photos:**
- The red glow or highlight on the problem zone should feel like a medical illustration — educational, not alarming.
- Consistent treatment across all cells (same color overlay, same opacity, same placement logic).
- Labels beneath each photo should be clean and legible at thumbnail size.

## Color Guidance

- **"Before" side / panels:** Slightly cooler, more desaturated treatment. The before state should feel heavier, duller.
- **"After" side / panels:** Warmer, brighter, more saturated. Relief feels like warmth and light.
- **Header:** Standard two-tone. Dark neutral base + product accent emphasis.
- **Day-count badge fill:** Product accent color.
- **Condition highlight overlays:** Warm red/coral — enough to indicate the problem zone without being medical-illustration harsh. Light opacity.
- **Product image:** Natural, clean. No color treatment — the product is the neutral solution between the problem and the after state.
- **Footer swatch strip:** Clean row of colored circles or rectangles — the actual product colorway hex values. Black outline or drop shadow on each swatch to separate from background.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Fake before photos.** Before state must look real. If it looks retouched or theatrical, the whole comparison loses credibility.
- **Before and after in different lighting.** If the lighting, crop, or angle changes between before and after, the comparison reads as dishonest.
- **Day count missing.** In the Before/After variant, the day badge is essential. Without it, the timeline is undefined and the claim is weaker.
- **"*Actual Customer Photos" disclaimer missing.** Required when using real customer imagery. Legal protection and authenticity signal.
- **Multi-Problem Grid without condition labels.** The labels name the specific conditions — that specificity is the persuasive point. Generic labels like "foot pain" defeat the purpose.
- **No product in the image.** All three variants must include the product. The product is the answer to the problem being shown.
